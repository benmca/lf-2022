**Cast the net out** 

The daily ebb and flow of the sounds crossing my path is like the surface of the sea, and this daily posting of sounds, it's like casting a net out. Sometimes I catch an idea or demo from an old notebook I've forgotten about, a work in progress, something finished I forgot about, or just something unexpected I hear on that day.

I started this daily sound journal for the energetic benefits, since one of my biggest energizers is completion. When a composition is done, I listen back to it and go "I made this, this is a new thing. It's different than what I've made before, and I'm moving forward". I'm developing. 

After the first 10 or 20 of these posts, I saw the progress, and a landscape came into view. I saw ideas that I'd shelved for weeks, months or years, and then I'd work on them some more, continually honing, and I'd chart development over time.

---

**Any sound I make**

I am an obsessive recorder, a sound hoarder. I have hours (days maybe) of sound recorded of my demos, works in progress, hummed snippets of melody I think worth keeping, and of course finished recordings I've released. At the outset of this project, I figured I could fill the minutes out just pulling from the archives, but what you'd typically think of as composing, songwriting, etc - that was not my only source of minutes.

Sometimes I'd go out in my environment and capture sound. I began to take sound overnight, the [biophony](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soundscape_ecology), and pulled together a portrait of the evening. I'd find some highlights, always trying to keep things to a minute–a brief sonic fingerprint of a particular day. 

When focused on learning a new instrument or developing a new technique, I found you generate a lot of sound when you practice and there are always things that kind of delighted me when I'd record my explorations of new territory. 

I began making cassette tape-pause compositions (it's really fun) by recording little tiny micro samples off the radio in a live collage, pausing and unpausing real quick instead of editing, culling sounds from the air, inspired by John Oswald's Mystery Tapes. I've got a 90 minute tape and it's about halfway full after 2 years. 

--- 

**Listen Faster**

I do a lot of listening. If you ask people who know me, they'll say listening is my strength. At one point, I rationalized using this name Listen Faster because I wanted to listen to everything. I wanted to perceive everything. I was hungry for new sounds. I saw this name on the front of an issue of Popular Science from the early '70s, and it resonated with me. I was so excited about all the sound that was out there, so energized by the thought that you could never hear it all. I thought "I'll never be bored. I'll never exhaust all the possibilities the world has with sound. Even if I lost my hearing at some point, I'd still hear things in my head." These thoughts grounded me.

**Critic, please be still**

In the moment, when that spark of creation hits, and you hear something you're doing that sounds like an idea worth pursuing, there's a critic in your head that says something like "not good enough", or "not worth exploring". In your forebrain you know not to pay any attention to that critic, but the critic can stick to you a gut level.

If I don't hit record–if the critic is not having it–the idea is lost to the sands of time. So in these minutes, sometimes you hear really sloppy attempts at what I hear in my head as a finished piece. I didn't have time to polish it that day, but I had to get it posted. A spectrum exists in these: from immediate utterances to the very developed. 

It felt risky like bungee-jumping at first, like I was going streaking by putting an idea up that wasn't finished. [My friend Rafał has a blog called Untested](https://untested.sonnet.io) - I love that title. These ideas are untested to me most of the time. I'm not sure if I like them or not, but I go ahead and put them up. Then I get some distance. I find a day or two later I may not even recognize it as my own thought, my own sound. I have such fresh ears on when I find that distance.

---

**Cultivating distance from the birth of an idea**

This is what I've gotten out of the daily practice. Cultivating that distance from the initial immediate thought, I see a lot more value in the different ways that I can capture a sound. I have a richer awareness of who I am and what tools do I have in my toolbox.

When I am in the moment with a group that I feel intimidated by, I may revert to somebody who thinks all they have is a hammer and a screwdriver in their toolbox, and I do really simple cliche things, usually not well. I will try to hurry things. I'll try to go through licks, things that so many people have heard.  I'll fall back on the derivative. This process shows me where I'm original, and not derivative. 

Listening to an idea over and over in your headphones in private is a different experience than listening to something when you're sharing it with somebody for the 1st time. The fresh open ears of others make my ears open differently. I started to look forward to this auditioning process after a while. I'd always get kind of a butterfly in my stomach, maybe just one, I would get just ever so slightly nervous about it. Like I needed to explain it, or I needed to qualify it, saying that this isn't done yet. This whole journaling process has started to kill that tension in me, and I've earned healthy thoughts, like, just let the piece of audio stand on its own, as opposed to me needing to qualify it.
